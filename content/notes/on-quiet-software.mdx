The best software I use is software I've stopped noticing. My notes app. My terminal. My text editor. They've receded into the background of how I work. I don't think about them, I think through them.

Most new software is the opposite. It wants my attention. It pings me, animates at me, gamifies the smallest actions, celebrates trivial successes, and nudges me back when I try to leave. It treats my attention as a resource to be mined.

## The tradeoff

There's a real argument for the loud approach: engagement metrics go up. Users open the app more. Growth charts look good. The CEO gets to say "record MAUs" on the earnings call.

But engagement and *usefulness* are different things. I open Slack more than any other app on my phone, and I get less value from it than from almost anything else on that home screen. The apps I value most, maps, music, notes, I open when I need them and close when I'm done.

## What quiet software looks like

- It does its job and gets out of the way.
- It doesn't invent reasons to notify you.
- It respects that you have other things to do.
- It's fast, because slow software is loud software.
- It doesn't try to become a "platform."

## Why I care

Because I'm building things, and I'd like them to be the kind of thing someone uses for ten minutes and forgets about for a week, then comes back to because they need it, not the kind of thing they have to delete off their phone to get a real night's sleep.

Quiet software is harder to build. It doesn't spike the engagement chart. It wins by being trusted, which takes years. But I'd rather build something trusted than something addictive.
