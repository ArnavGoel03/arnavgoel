The supplement industry is big, loud, and mostly full of noise. Most products are either unnecessary, underdosed, or both. Before I try a new supplement, I run it through three questions.

## 1. Is there a deficiency I can measure?

If I'm thinking about vitamin D, I test my 25-OH vitamin D before I buy the bottle. If I'm thinking about magnesium, I look at whether my RBC magnesium is in a reasonable range. If I can't point to a number that's low, I skip it.

The exception is supplements where "deficient" isn't quite the right frame, omega-3s, for instance, where the American diet is systematically low.

## 2. Is the evidence good?

"Good" to me means more than one randomized trial, published in a serious journal, with an effect size that isn't trivial. Not PubMed-Googling until I find a study that agrees with me. Not podcast-bro testimonials.

For most supplements the answer is: weak or mixed. That's usually enough to pass.

## 3. Is the dose right?

A lot of supplements have legitimate ingredients at laughably small doses. Proprietary blends are a red flag, they exist to hide underdosing. If the research uses 400mg of something and the product has 50mg, I'm paying for the idea of the supplement, not the supplement.

## The result

Most things don't pass. The ones that do, I write up on the [supplements](/supplements) page, and the ones I tried that didn't work are on that page too, because knowing what *not* to buy is often more useful than knowing what to buy.
