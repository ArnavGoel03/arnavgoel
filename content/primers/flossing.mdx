## The "flossing isn't proven" myth

In 2016, the AP ran an article pointing out that the government's Dietary Guidelines had removed flossing recommendations due to "weak evidence." The nuance got lost. What the review actually found:

- Most flossing trials are short (4-12 weeks).
- Self-reported flossing is unreliable.
- Controlled trials comparing "brushing + flossing" vs "brushing alone" show modest but measurable benefit.

The real-world signal is clearer. Any dentist who sees hundreds of mouths a year can identify which patients floss regularly and which don't within 30 seconds of looking at interdental gum tissue.

**Flossing works when done consistently.** The evidence gap is about trial design, not biology. Plaque and food debris accumulate between teeth where a toothbrush physically can't reach; removing that debris prevents interproximal cavities and gingivitis in the interdental papillae.

## String floss

### What it does best
- Precision: physically scrapes the tooth surface below the contact point.
- Cheapest, most portable tool.
- Good for tight contacts (teeth close together).

### Technique that actually works

1. **18 inches of floss.** Wind around the middle finger of each hand.
2. **Between thumbs and index fingers**, work a 2-inch section.
3. **Slide between teeth gently.** Don't snap.
4. **C-shape around each tooth.** Hug one tooth, then the other. You're scraping sides, not just going up-and-down.
5. **Fresh section of floss for each gap.** Don't re-use a dirty section.

### Forms
- **Waxed vs unwaxed.** Waxed glides through tight contacts more easily. Unwaxed is slightly more abrasive, which some dentists prefer.
- **PTFE (Glide-style).** Won't shred. Good for crowded teeth.
- **Floss picks.** Convenient and better than nothing, but the fixed-string limits the C-shape technique.
- **Tape floss.** Thicker, good for wider gaps.

## Water picks (oral irrigators)

### What they do best
- Flush food debris and loose plaque from pockets and between teeth.
- Reach areas around braces, bridges, implants, and dental work.
- Easier for people with arthritic hands, large fingers, or poor dexterity.

### Evidence
- Water picks reduce gingival bleeding more than string floss in most head-to-head trials.
- String floss is more effective for plaque specifically in the interdental contact area.
- Combined: a water pick + string floss is the gold standard for periodontal maintenance.

### Technique
1. **Lean over the sink.** This is a splash sport.
2. **Lowest comfortable pressure setting to start.** Work up.
3. **Aim at the gum line at a 90-degree angle.**
4. **Pause at each gap for 1-2 seconds.**
5. **Warm water feels better.** Your gums don't enjoy cold.

### Models worth buying
- **Waterpik Aquarius (countertop):** the standard. Loud, works well.
- **Waterpik Cordless Advanced:** smaller, travel-friendly, 3 minutes of battery work time.
- **Philips Sonicare Power Flosser:** sleeker form factor, comparable performance.

Skip unbranded knockoffs. The pump mechanisms fail.

## Interdental brushes

### What they do best
- Fill the gap between teeth when it's too wide for floss and too narrow for a toothpick.
- Great for dental work (bridges, implants, orthodontic expanders).
- Fast: a single pass through each gap takes seconds.

### Sizing
Comes in multiple diameters (0.4 mm for tight gaps up to 1.5 mm+ for wider). A dentist or hygienist can tell you which size fits which gap. Using the wrong size: too small wastes time, too big damages the gum papilla.

### Brands
- **TePe interdental brushes:** the gold standard. Color-coded by size.
- **Oral-B Interdental Brushes:** widely available.
- **GUM Soft-Picks:** rubberized tips, gentler, less effective for plaque.

## Which tool for which mouth

### Default (most people):
**String floss** daily + **brushing twice a day**. Period. That's the 80/20.

### If you have:
- **Braces, bridges, implants, crowns:** Water pick is transformative. Use daily alongside floss.
- **Wide gaps between teeth:** Interdental brushes.
- **Gum recession / larger embrasures:** Interdental brushes first, floss second.
- **Tight contacts and crowded teeth:** Waxed or PTFE floss; water pick as adjunct.
- **Arthritis or dexterity issues:** Water pick as primary tool; floss when able.

### Periodontal disease or deeper pockets:
Your dentist or periodontist should prescribe a specific regimen. A combination of floss, water pick, and interdental brushes is common. Chlorhexidine rinse or prescription toothpaste may be added.

## Frequency

**Once a day, minimum.** Twice daily gives slightly more benefit but compliance collapses; most people do 1-2 minutes total of interdental work once daily and that's the realistic target.

**Any time of day** works. Before bed tends to be the most effective because bacteria sit undisturbed overnight.

## What to skip

- **Single-use floss picks as your only tool.** Fine for travel, inadequate for daily use.
- **"Floss brush" hybrids.** Gimmicky.
- **Air floss (the Sonicare kind that uses bursts of air/water).** Less effective than a proper water pick. Discontinued in most markets.

## What makes flossing actually stick

- **Leave the floss on the counter.** Not in a drawer. Sight is the biggest adherence driver.
- **Floss in front of the mirror.** Seeing the technique improves it.
- **Combine with another habit.** Right after brushing, every time.

## The bar

If you floss once a day consistently, you're in the top quartile of oral hygiene. The tool doesn't matter nearly as much as showing up. Pick whichever one you'll actually use.
