## Why this matters

Two supplements with identical marketing can deliver entirely different amounts of the actual active ingredient. A $40 "sleep support" blend might have 50 mg of the ingredient that works, while a $15 single-ingredient version has 300 mg. The label tells you which is which, if you know what to read.

## The "Supplement Facts" panel

In the US, every supplement is legally required to print a Supplement Facts panel showing:

- Serving size
- Servings per container
- Each active ingredient with its **amount per serving**
- **Daily Value** percentage (if an established DV exists)

Key things to check:

### 1. Amount per serving

The ingredient you care about needs to be at a **clinically effective dose**. Examples:

- **Ashwagandha (KSM-66):** 300-600 mg. Below 200 mg is sub-clinical.
- **Magnesium (elemental):** 300-400 mg. "Magnesium" on the label can be listed as elemental or as total salt weight; always check.
- **Creatine monohydrate:** 3-5 g.
- **Omega-3 (EPA + DHA combined):** 1-4 g for cardiovascular benefit.
- **Ashwagandha generic root powder:** 3-5 g (much higher than extract because of lower withanolide density).

If the label doesn't specify elemental weight for minerals (magnesium, zinc, iron, calcium), the dose is ambiguous.

### 2. "Proprietary blend" is a red flag

When a label says "SleepBlend™ 500 mg" and lists the ingredients underneath without individual amounts, you have no idea how much of each is in there. This is a **proprietary blend**.

Regulatory fact: the ingredients in a proprietary blend are listed in **descending order by weight**. The first ingredient is the most abundant; the last is the least. That tells you something, but not enough.

What a proprietary blend usually means:
- A tiny amount of an expensive "hero" ingredient (enough to list it on the front label).
- A bulk of cheaper filler ingredients to hit the blend's total weight.

**Rule:** if the active ingredients you care about are in a proprietary blend rather than listed separately with amounts, assume the clinical dose is not present. Shop elsewhere.

## "Other ingredients" tells you about quality

Below the Supplement Facts panel, there's a smaller section labeled "Other ingredients." This lists everything that isn't an active: the capsule, fillers, flow agents, preservatives.

Signals to note:

### Red flags
- **Magnesium stearate (in excess).** Small amounts as a flow agent are fine. Dominant inclusion is a cheap-manufacturing sign.
- **Titanium dioxide.** Now banned in EU food; still in US supplements as a whitening agent. Reasonable evidence for genotoxicity; avoid if you can.
- **Artificial colors** in a capsule you swallow immediately. Pointless.
- **Seed oils (soybean, cottonseed)** in softgels. Fine if minor; inappropriate if the softgel *is* primarily seed oil with a trace active.
- **Carrageenan.** Possible inflammatory effects in sensitive people.

### Neutral-to-positive
- **Silicon dioxide / rice flour** as flow agent or filler: fine.
- **Gelatin** or **veggie caps (HPMC, pullulan):** just encapsulation.
- **Ascorbyl palmitate / vitamin E** as antioxidant: common and fine.
- **MCT oil or olive oil in softgels:** good carrier.

### A product with a "Supplement Facts" that only lists the hero ingredients and a "Other ingredients" line that's three pages long is paying to hide what's in the capsule.

## Daily Value (%DV) isn't always useful

The FDA's Daily Value is set for preventing deficiency, not for therapeutic effect. Vitamin D at 100% DV (20 mcg or 800 IU) prevents rickets in a child; it doesn't get most adults to a blood level of 40-60 ng/mL.

Use %DV as a sanity check for basics (protein, fiber, total vitamins), not as a gauge of therapeutic dose for functional supplements.

## Third-party testing

The FDA does not pre-approve supplements for purity or potency. Independent testing is how you get that assurance:

- **NSF Certified for Sport.** The most rigorous. Tests for potency + banned substances. Required for competitive athletes. Thorne, some Sports Research SKUs.
- **NSF Certified.** Non-sport version. Still meaningful.
- **USP Verified.** Pharmaceutical-level potency testing. Common on mainstream brands.
- **Informed Choice / Informed Sport.** Banned-substance testing without full potency verification.
- **ConsumerLab.com / Labdoor.** Independent after-market tests. Results available by subscription. Useful for checking brands you already use.

**Rule:** if spending more than $30/month on a supplement, choose a third-party-tested version. The price premium is typically 20-40% and the quality assurance is real.

## Extract standardization

For herbal products, standardization to an active compound matters:

- **Ashwagandha KSM-66:** standardized to 5% withanolides.
- **Turmeric / curcumin:** standardized to 95% curcuminoids (Longvida, Meriva, C3 Complex are the bioavailable forms).
- **Rhodiola rosea:** standardized to 3% rosavins and 1% salidrosides.
- **Ginseng:** standardized to ginsenoside content.

If the label says "500 mg of ashwagandha root" without a standardization percentage or a specific extract name, you're buying ground root of unknown potency.

## Bioavailability claims

"Enhanced absorption" is a popular marketing claim. Some versions are real:

- **Curcumin + piperine (BioPerine)** or phytosome form (Meriva, Longvida): genuinely better absorbed.
- **Liposomal vitamin C / glutathione:** some evidence, expensive.
- **Methylated B vitamins** for MTHFR variants: relevant for a subset of people.

Most "nano-delivery," "enhanced bioavailability," and "liposomal" claims on mid-tier products are marketing without clinical backing.

## The 60-second checklist

Before buying any supplement:

1. Is the dose of the key active at clinical levels? (Not in a proprietary blend, listed separately, at a dose that matches published trials.)
2. Is there a third-party certification? (NSF, USP, Informed Sport.)
3. Does the "Other ingredients" line look reasonable? (No hidden fillers, no titanium dioxide if you care, no excessive stabilizers.)
4. For herbal products: is it a standardized extract? (KSM-66 Sensoril Longvida Meriva or equivalent, not raw ground root.)
5. Cost per clinical dose, not per bottle.

If a product fails 1 or 2, move on. If it fails 3, consider cheaper alternatives. If it fails 4 (on herbals), it's almost certainly underdosed.

## What to look for on the back label

- Manufacturer's physical address, not a PO box.
- Lot number and expiration date.
- Storage instructions (stable compounds don't need refrigeration; fish oil does).
- Clear allergen disclosures.

Transparency is a quality signal. Brands that hide basic manufacturing info usually have reasons.
