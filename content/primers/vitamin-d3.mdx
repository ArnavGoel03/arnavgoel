## What it is

Vitamin D3 (cholecalciferol) is the form your skin produces when UVB hits 7-dehydrocholesterol. It isn't really a vitamin in the classical sense; it's a prohormone your liver and kidneys convert to calcitriol (1,25-dihydroxyvitamin D), which acts on vitamin D receptors all over the body: bone, gut, immune cells, skeletal muscle, even parts of the brain.

Vitamin D2 (ergocalciferol, from plants and fungi) is weaker at raising blood 25-hydroxyvitamin D and less bioavailable. For supplementation, D3 is the answer.

## Who is deficient

Most people living above 35 degrees latitude in the winter. Most people with darker skin at any latitude. Most office workers anywhere. Most people over 65. Some estimates put US insufficiency (under 30 ng/mL 25-OH-D) at 40% of the adult population.

You don't have to guess. A 25-hydroxyvitamin D blood test costs about $40 without insurance and gives you a number to work against.

## The target

- **Deficiency:** under 20 ng/mL (50 nmol/L). Associated with measurable bone, immune, and mood effects.
- **Insufficient:** 20 to 30 ng/mL.
- **Sufficient:** 30 to 100 ng/mL.
- **Optimal** (where most of the positive outcome data sits): **40 to 60 ng/mL (100-150 nmol/L)**.
- **Toxicity** does not occur below about 150 ng/mL even at high doses, though ambition to overshoot is not useful.

## Dosing

Most adults need **2,000 to 4,000 IU/day** to reach and maintain the 40-60 ng/mL range. People with darker skin, obesity, or malabsorption conditions may need 5,000-10,000 IU/day. Go by blood level, not by a generic dose.

**Take with a fat-containing meal.** Vitamin D is fat-soluble; absorption drops substantially on an empty stomach.

## Why it pairs with K2

When you raise circulating vitamin D, you increase calcium absorption from the gut. Where that calcium ends up matters:

- **With enough vitamin K2:** calcium gets deposited in bone and teeth.
- **Without enough vitamin K2:** some of that calcium ends up in arterial walls and soft tissue, which is the opposite of the longevity effect you wanted.

Pair 2,000-4,000 IU D3 with 100-200 mcg K2 (MK-7 form, long-acting). Same meal.

The evidence for the K2 pairing sits in observational studies (Rotterdam Heart Study, Dutch population cohorts) more than in RCTs, but the mechanism is clean and K2 is cheap. Take it.

## What supplementation meaningfully does (when you're insufficient)

- Reduces fracture risk in older adults
- Reduces respiratory infection frequency, modestly
- Improves grip strength and fall rates in the elderly
- Corrects the mood signal associated with deficiency (seasonal depression component)
- Supports normal parathyroid and calcium homeostasis

## What it does less of than marketing suggests (when you're already sufficient)

- Acute cold and flu prevention is real but small effect-size.
- Cancer prevention: the data is mixed and weaker than claims imply.
- Depression treatment is limited to deficient populations; sufficient people don't gain.

## The sun question

Sun exposure is the evolutionarily correct way to get vitamin D. 10-20 minutes of bright midday sun on uncovered skin (arms, legs, back) in summer at latitudes below 35 can produce 10,000+ IU of D3. Caveats:

- You need UVB, which is blocked at lower sun angles (winter, early morning, late afternoon).
- Window glass blocks UVB.
- SPF 30+ blocks ~97% of UVB. If you wear sunscreen on your face but have forearms exposed, you still get plenty.
- Melanin protects against burning but also slows D3 synthesis.

Sun + a smaller supplemental dose is better than either alone for most people.

## Buying notes

Don't overthink it. D3 is a commodity. Any reputable softgel with 2,000-5,000 IU per capsule, paired with a K2 capsule, will do. NOW Foods, Thorne, Sports Research: all fine. Third-party testing matters less here than for most supplements because D3 is a simple molecule.
