## The two technologies

Every electric toothbrush on the market uses one of two mechanisms:

### Oscillating-rotating (Oral-B)

The brush head is circular. It spins left-right at 7,000-10,000 movements per minute, sometimes with an added pulsation (back-forward) that knocks plaque loose. Oral-B has owned this category since 1998; every "iO" and "Pro" series uses some version of it.

### Sonic (Philips Sonicare, and most non-Oral-B brands)

The brush head is elongated. It vibrates side-to-side at 30,000-62,000 movements per minute. The high speed creates fluid dynamics that help sweep plaque from areas the bristles don't directly touch.

## Which works better

The Cochrane review (the gold-standard meta-analysis in dentistry) puts it clearly:

- **Both electric families outperform manual brushing** by 11-21% on plaque reduction.
- **Oscillating-rotating has a slight edge over sonic** in head-to-head short-term trials on plaque and gingivitis.
- **The difference is small** in properly-conducted long-term trials. Technique and consistency matter more than brush type.

Pragmatic takeaway: either technology, used for 2 minutes twice daily with correct technique, is meaningfully better than a manual toothbrush. Pick by feel, price, and feature set.

## The pressure sensor (underrated)

The single most useful non-motor feature on modern electric toothbrushes. A ring around the neck of the brush shines red (and some models throttle the motor) when you're pressing too hard.

Over-brushing is real. Common consequences:

- **Gum recession.** Gums pull back from the tooth, exposing sensitive root surfaces.
- **Enamel abrasion.** Wears the enamel cervical to the gum line.
- **Dentin hypersensitivity.** The exposed root surface has no enamel coating; cold water hurts.

A pressure sensor fixes a habit you may not know you have. Worth paying for.

Tiers:
- **Oral-B iO series:** visible pressure ring + motor throttling. Best implementation.
- **Oral-B Pro series (3000, 5000, 7000, etc.):** visible pressure warning light. Good.
- **Sonicare ExpertClean / DiamondClean:** pressure sensor with haptic feedback.
- **Budget electric brushes:** no pressure sensor. Skip if you can afford the upgrade.

## The 2-minute timer (underrated, part two)

Almost no one actually brushes for 2 minutes on a manual brush. Self-reports average 45-70 seconds. The difference matters: plaque and biofilm need contact time to disrupt.

Electric brushes have a built-in 2-minute timer and usually a 30-second quadrant pulse (telling you to switch zones). Once you've used one, you realize how brief 30 seconds is, and how often you were previously giving each quadrant 10 seconds.

## The app (mostly skip)

Modern brushes (Oral-B iO, Sonicare ExpertClean) ship with Bluetooth connectivity and a companion app. It maps your brushing coverage and scores technique.

**Useful once.** Interesting as a diagnostic tool for the first few sessions. Tedious on daily basis. Most users turn off Bluetooth within a week.

Don't pay a premium for app features. The pressure sensor and timer are what matter.

## Brush heads

Heads wear out. Replace every **3 months** or when the bristles visibly splay.

- **Soft bristles only.** Medium and hard bristles damage gums and enamel. If your brand only sells medium heads, switch brands.
- **Round (Oral-B) vs rectangular (Sonicare)**: personal preference. Round is easier for small mouths and molars.
- **Specialty heads:** interdental heads for people with braces or wide gum pockets. Charcoal heads are cosmetic marketing. White-bristle "whitening" heads are marketing; they do nothing a regular head doesn't.

## Technique

The brush does the work. Your job is positioning:

1. **Hold the brush at 45 degrees to the gum line.**
2. **Let it glide; don't scrub.** The oscillation is the scrub.
3. **Spend 30 seconds per quadrant.** Outer surfaces, inner surfaces, chewing surfaces.
4. **Don't forget the tongue.** Biofilm on the tongue is a major source of breath issues.
5. **Rinse and spit, don't over-rinse.** Leave a thin film of fluoride or hydroxyapatite on the teeth.

## Travel

Most modern electric brushes have 2-3 week battery life on a single charge. Travel versions exist but add cost. A fresh charge before a 10-day trip is usually enough.

## Who should skip electric

Almost no one. The one exception: people with certain tremors or motor-control issues where the vibration is unmanageable. Most dentists will disagree and suggest a weighted electric brush handle.

## The honest summary

Buy any brush with:
1. Pressure sensor (non-negotiable once you've had one).
2. 2-minute timer with 30-second pulses.
3. Soft-bristle heads available widely (so replacements are easy).

Everything else is nice-to-have. Oral-B iO Series 5 or Sonicare ExpertClean 7300 are the sweet spots for value. Anything above that is paying for incremental app features and marketing.
