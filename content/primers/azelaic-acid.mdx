## What it is

Azelaic acid is a nine-carbon dicarboxylic acid naturally produced by Malassezia yeast on skin. It's been used medically for decades (15% cream is FDA-approved for rosacea; 20% for acne). It works through multiple mechanisms at once, which is why it's one of the most underrated multi-tool ingredients in skincare.

## What it actually does

### 1. Anti-inflammatory
Suppresses the neutrophil activity that produces visible redness in rosacea and inflammatory acne. This is the primary reason dermatologists prescribe it for rosacea.

### 2. Tyrosinase inhibition
Selectively inhibits tyrosinase in **hyperactive** melanocytes (the ones causing melasma or PIH) without affecting normal pigmentation. That's why it fades dark spots without lightening the surrounding skin, which sets it apart from hydroquinone.

### 3. Mildly comedolytic
Normalizes keratinization in pore linings, preventing the cell buildup that forms blackheads and whiteheads.

### 4. Antibacterial
Direct activity against C. acnes bacteria. Not as strong as benzoyl peroxide, but without the bleaching and irritation.

One molecule, four jobs. Which is why it appears in so many dermatology protocols.

## Concentrations

- **10% (The Ordinary's Azelaic Acid Suspension, Paula's Choice Azelaic Acid Booster).** OTC, entry-level. Decent results; longer to see effects.
- **15% Finacea (prescription in the US).** Rosacea workhorse.
- **20% Azelex (prescription in the US).** Acne-first.
- **Azeclair-style dermatology compounds (up to 30%).** Compounding pharmacies can go higher for stubborn melasma.

For most people, **10% OTC is plenty** to see results over 8-12 weeks. If you're not responding, your dermatologist can prescribe 15-20%.

## Who should use it

### Rosacea or persistent redness
The single most effective topical for reducing rosacea flush without the rebound-dilation risk of topical steroids. Works slowly; commit to 3 months.

### Melasma
Pair with oral or topical tranexamic acid, strict sunscreen, and niacinamide. Azelaic is the gentlest tyrosinase inhibitor and doesn't cause the ochronosis risk of hydroquinone.

### Post-inflammatory hyperpigmentation (PIH)
The dark spots that linger after a pimple heals. Azelaic fades these while preventing new ones.

### Mild acne
Especially the comedonal and mild inflammatory types. For severe cystic acne, it's a supporting actor; benzoyl peroxide, retinoids, or systemic treatment lead.

### Sensitive skin that can't tolerate retinoid
Azelaic gives overlapping benefits with much less irritation and zero retinization phase.

## How to use it

- **Frequency:** start every other morning or every other evening. Build up to once or twice daily.
- **Before or after moisturizer:** thin, active serum first (after cleansing), moisturizer on top.
- **Morning or evening:** either works. Not photosensitizing (unlike AHAs and retinoids).
- **With SPF daily.** Not because of azelaic, but because you're treating pigmentation, which will return if you let UV through.

## Side effects

- **Tingling or stinging** on application for the first few uses. Usually dissipates.
- **Mild flaking or dryness** early on; usually resolves within 2-3 weeks.
- **Worsening before improving** for some people, especially with acne. Push through 4-6 weeks.

## What it pairs well with

- **Niacinamide.** Synergistic for redness, barrier support, and pigmentation.
- **Sunscreen.** Always.
- **Gentle moisturizer with ceramides.** Supports the barrier while azelaic does its work.
- **Tranexamic acid.** The classic melasma co-treatment.

## What to be cautious with

- **Strong AHAs or retinoid on the same night.** Azelaic is mild, but stacking actives can push the barrier past its limit. Alternate days or times.
- **Benzoyl peroxide in the same application window.** Not harmful, but redundant; azelaic already has antibacterial action. Pick one or alternate.

## Realistic expectations

- Redness: noticeably calmer at 6-8 weeks.
- PIH and melasma: visibly lighter at 8-12 weeks, significantly lighter at 4-6 months.
- Acne: fewer new lesions by week 6-8.

Azelaic is the "slow and steady" active. It doesn't give you a dramatic transformation in two weeks. What it gives you is fewer flare-ups, calmer skin, and steady improvement over months, without the irritation budget that stronger actives eat.
