## What it is

Hyaluronic acid (HA) is a glycosaminoglycan, a sugar-chain humectant that naturally makes up a big part of your skin's extracellular matrix. One molecule can bind up to a thousand times its weight in water, which is the stat every marketing page cites.

What the marketing usually leaves out: whether it actually hydrates your skin depends on **what size molecule you're applying** and **what the humidity of the air is**.

## Molecular weight changes everything

HA molecules in skincare range across four orders of magnitude. Each size does a different job:

- **Very low molecular weight HA (≤10 kDa, sometimes called "oligo-HA").** Tiny. Penetrates into the deeper epidermis and upper dermis. Signals and hydrates at depth. Some concern about mild pro-inflammatory signaling at very low MW, but well-formulated products handle this.
- **Low MW HA (10-50 kDa).** Penetrates the epidermis. Plumps from within.
- **Medium MW HA (50-500 kDa).** Sits on the skin surface, holds water topically. Most common in budget products.
- **High MW HA (500 kDa+, up to 2 MDa).** Forms a film on the skin surface. Occlusive-like effect, smoothing and hydration that's more external than internal.

**Cross-linked HA** is a special case: chemically bonded chains that form gel matrices. Common in injectable fillers. In topicals, cross-linked HA is used for time-release surface hydration.

The best HA serums use **a blend of molecular weights**. A product that only lists "hyaluronic acid" without specifying MW is almost certainly medium or high MW only. The SNP-label products that list "multi-weight HA" or specify the kDa ranges tend to work better.

## The humidity problem

Humectants work by pulling water toward themselves. Where the water comes from depends on where water is available.

- **In high humidity (60%+):** HA pulls water from the air into your skin. Net hydration gain.
- **In low humidity (30% or less):** HA pulls water from deeper in your skin upward, where it evaporates. If you don't seal that in with an occlusive moisturizer, you end up drier than when you started.

Practical rule: **always seal HA serum with a moisturizer** containing lipids (ceramides, cholesterol, fatty acids, or petrolatum). This is especially important if you live in a dry climate or spend your day in air conditioning.

The single biggest mistake people make with HA is applying it as a final step and walking away. That's how "hyaluronic acid made my skin drier" happens.

## Application technique

- **Apply to damp skin.** Right after cleansing, before the skin fully dries. This gives the HA water molecules to grab onto from the surface.
- **Layer moisturizer on top immediately.** Don't wait for the HA to fully absorb; that defeats the purpose.
- **Pat, don't rub.** HA serums are often gel-textured and ball up under friction.

## Dose

Most HA serums are 1-2% HA. That's enough. "10% HA" is marketing; the molecule is already so active at sub-percent concentrations that higher loadings don't proportionally increase hydration.

## What it actually does (reasonable expectations)

- **Immediate:** Plumped, dewy appearance within 10-30 minutes after application.
- **Short-term (1-2 weeks):** Measurable increase in corneal hydration.
- **Long-term (2-3 months):** Some evidence for improved elasticity with consistent use, particularly with low-MW formulations.
- **Wrinkle "filling":** Short-term optical effect (smoother surface), not true wrinkle reversal.

## What it doesn't do

- **Collagen synthesis.** HA doesn't signal fibroblasts the way retinoids or vitamin C do. It's a hydrator, not a remodeler.
- **Anti-inflammatory action.** Mild at best.
- **Substitute for occlusive moisturizers** in dry climates. HA without an occlusive seal is incomplete hydration.

## Who benefits most

- Dehydrated skin of any type (even oily skin can be dehydrated).
- Skin that looks dull and tired despite regular moisturizing.
- Post-retinoid / post-acid evenings, to rehydrate the barrier.
- Humid climates, where the air is cooperating.

## Who benefits less

- Someone with genuinely dry skin (low sebum, compromised barrier) needs ceramides and occlusives more than they need HA.
- Someone in very dry climates should use HA always under a heavier moisturizer, not as a standalone.

## The only rule

HA is a hydration step, not a hydration finish. Layer it under your moisturizer, not on top.
