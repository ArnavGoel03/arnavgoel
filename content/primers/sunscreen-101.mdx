## The single most important skincare product

If you can only use one skincare product every day, use sunscreen. The visible signs of aging most people try to reverse with expensive serums (hyperpigmentation, wrinkles, textural damage, loss of firmness) are overwhelmingly caused by cumulative UV exposure. Daily broad-spectrum sunscreen is the closest thing to an anti-aging product that actually works.

## What the UV spectrum does

- **UVB (290-320 nm):** penetrates the epidermis. Causes sunburn, tans, direct DNA damage, skin cancer (basal cell, squamous cell, melanoma). Stronger mid-day, weaker morning and evening, blocked by window glass.
- **UVA (320-400 nm):** penetrates deeper into the dermis. Causes the visible signs of aging (wrinkles, elastosis, pigmentation, sagging). **Not blocked by window glass.** Present all day, year-round, even on cloudy days.

SPF numbers measure UVB protection. They say nothing about UVA. That's where "broad-spectrum" matters.

## SPF math (what the numbers actually mean)

SPF is a multiplier of the time it would take to burn without protection.

- **SPF 15** blocks ~93% of UVB.
- **SPF 30** blocks ~97%.
- **SPF 50** blocks ~98%.
- **SPF 100** blocks ~99%.

The percentages cluster because of the math (1/SPF is the fraction transmitted). Going from 30 to 50 is a small jump in blocking UVB, but the **margin of error matters**: most people apply a fraction of the tested dose (2 mg/cm²). In real life, labeled SPF 50 often delivers closer to SPF 30 because of thin application.

Practical rule: **aim for SPF 30 or above, applied generously**. Going above SPF 50 doesn't buy meaningful incremental protection if you apply correctly.

## UVA protection ratings

SPF doesn't tell you about UVA. Look for:

- **PA+++, PA++++ (Asian labeling).** More pluses = more UVA protection.
- **"Broad-spectrum" (US labeling).** The FDA's broad-spectrum test ensures a minimum UVA ratio but isn't granular.
- **UVA circle (EU labeling).** A circle with "UVA" inside indicates UVA protection is at least 1/3 of the labeled SPF.

Japanese and Korean sunscreens often have the best UVA filters (newer-generation molecules like Uvinul A Plus, Tinosorb S, Tinosorb M), which aren't all approved in the US yet.

## Mineral vs chemical

**Mineral (physical) filters:**
- **Zinc oxide** and **titanium dioxide**. Sit on top of skin and reflect/scatter UV (mostly they absorb, but slightly differently than chemical filters).
- Pros: stable, gentle, safe in pregnancy, good for sensitive and rosacea-prone skin, start working immediately.
- Cons: can leave a white cast, especially on deeper skin tones. Less cosmetically elegant unless the formulation is very good (tinted versions help). Usually cap out at SPF 30-50.

**Chemical (organic) filters:**
- **Avobenzone, octinoxate, octisalate, homosalate, oxybenzone** (older US), **Tinosorb S/M, Uvinul A Plus** (newer, EU/Asia).
- Pros: cosmetically elegant, easier high SPF, no white cast.
- Cons: some older filters are unstable in sun (avobenzone) and need co-stabilizers. Oxybenzone is reef-toxic and has estrogenic concerns; avoid it. Takes 15-20 minutes to start working.

**My default:** a new-generation chemical sunscreen from a Korean, Japanese, or European brand for daily elegance (Beauty of Joseon, Anessa, La Roche-Posay). A tinted mineral from a dermatology brand (La Roche-Posay Anthelios, EltaMD UV Clear) for sensitive days or post-procedure.

## Reapplication

Labeled SPF assumes initial application + reapplication every 2 hours if you're outside or sweating. For a desk job with limited sun exposure, once in the morning is usually enough. For:

- Outdoor activity, beach, skiing: reapply every 2 hours.
- Commute + office: once in the morning, fine.
- Driving a lot: reapply at lunch (cars transmit UVA).

## How much to apply

**Two fingers' worth (two full index-to-middle finger strokes of product)** for face and neck. That's roughly 1/4 teaspoon, which is more than most people use.

If you find that uncomfortable, the problem is usually the sunscreen; a better-formulated one doesn't feel heavy at correct application.

## Common mistakes

- **Thinking "I don't burn, so I don't need sunscreen."** UVA causes the cosmetic damage you care about. Burning is a UVB story.
- **Skipping on cloudy days.** Clouds block UVB better than UVA. You still get photoaging.
- **Relying on makeup SPF.** SPF 15 powder at a pea-sized dose gives you SPF 2-4. Use real sunscreen underneath.
- **Assuming "natural" means safer.** Carrot seed oil is not a sunscreen. Zinc oxide is the only plant-world-adjacent filter, and it's a mineral.

## The single rule

Wear it every day, face and neck, two fingers' worth, above SPF 30, broad-spectrum. Everything else in your skincare routine matters less.
