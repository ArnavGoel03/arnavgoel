## What it is

Melatonin is a hormone secreted by the pineal gland in response to darkness. It's the body's primary signal that "it's night." The dose that naturally circulates at peak is around 0.1 to 0.3 nanomoles per liter of plasma, which corresponds to a roughly 0.1 to 0.3 mg oral equivalent (with 10-15% bioavailability variance).

Over-the-counter melatonin in the US routinely comes in **3, 5, and 10 mg** tablets. That's **10 to 100 times** the physiologically active dose. A 2023 JAMA study found actual content in OTC gummies ranging from 74% to 347% of the label claim.

This is not a supplement where "more" is better. In most studies where researchers compared doses, the smallest (0.3 to 0.5 mg) worked best for sleep onset and had the fewest side effects.

## What it actually does

- **Shifts your circadian phase.** Taken in the evening, it tells your internal clock "night is coming." Useful for jet lag and for delayed-sleep-phase people.
- **Shortens sleep onset latency.** Modestly. About 7 minutes faster time to sleep in the average trial, which is real but not dramatic.
- **Does not deepen sleep.** Melatonin is a timing signal, not a sedative. If you're expecting benzodiazepine-like knockout, you'll be disappointed.

## What it doesn't do (despite marketing)

- It doesn't "fix" insomnia as a sedative would. If you have actual insomnia (difficulty falling asleep most nights despite good sleep hygiene), melatonin is the wrong tool.
- It doesn't reliably help children without a specific circadian-rhythm disorder. Pediatric melatonin use has exploded without strong efficacy evidence outside specific conditions (autism-spectrum sleep disorder, blind patients).
- It doesn't provide a hangover effect like alcohol, but high doses (5-10 mg) leave many people groggy the next morning.

## Dose

- **0.3 to 0.5 mg, 30 to 90 minutes before the desired sleep time.** This is the dose with the strongest clinical data.
- **1 mg max** for most purposes.
- **3 mg+ only for specific applications under a clinician's guidance** (e.g., blind patients, some shift workers).

The problem: you can't easily buy 0.3 mg tablets in the US. The smallest common dose is 1 mg. Options:

- Cut a 1 mg tablet in quarters.
- Buy pharmaceutical-grade 0.3 mg capsules (Life Extension, Douglas Labs, a few others).
- Order from a compounding pharmacy with a prescription.

## When to take it

**Good uses:**
- Eastbound travel, 2-8 time zones. Take it at the destination's bedtime for 2-3 nights.
- Westbound, less useful; your cue is morning light, not evening.
- Shift work rotation, with specific timing protocols.
- Non-24-hour sleep-wake disorder (most common in blind individuals).
- A one-off night when you need to sleep earlier than your natural drift.

**Bad uses:**
- Every night as a sleep aid. Better to address the upstream cause.
- At 5-10 mg doses.
- For acute stress-induced insomnia.

## Side effects worth knowing

- **Vivid or fragmented dreams.** Common at higher doses.
- **Morning grogginess.** Common at 3-10 mg doses, rare at 0.3-0.5 mg.
- **Downregulation of endogenous melatonin** with chronic high-dose use. Reversible but worth avoiding.
- **Interaction with anticoagulants and some psychiatric medications.** Check with a pharmacist if you're on multiple meds.

## What to do instead, for most insomnia

1. **Bright light** within an hour of waking, 10+ minutes outside.
2. **Zero caffeine after noon** if you're sensitive, 2 pm maximum for anyone.
3. **Last meal 3 hours before bed.**
4. **Cool, dark room.** 65-68°F.
5. **Consistent wake time** including weekends.

These do more than any dose of melatonin for long-run sleep quality. Use melatonin as a tool for specific problems (travel, shift changes, rare off-schedule nights), not a daily habit.
