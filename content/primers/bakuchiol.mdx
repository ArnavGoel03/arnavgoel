## What it is

Bakuchiol is a meroterpene extracted from the seeds of *Psoralea corylifolia* (Babchi), a plant with a long history in Ayurvedic medicine. It became a skincare darling around 2018 when a small comparison trial suggested it was "as effective as retinol" with fewer side effects.

The phrase "natural retinol alternative" is now attached to almost every bakuchiol product on the market. The claim is not nothing. It's also not what you'd think from the marketing.

## The science, honestly

Bakuchiol activates some of the same gene-expression pathways as retinoids (notably type I, III, and IV collagen and regulators of matrix metalloproteinases). It doesn't bind retinoic-acid receptors directly; it just produces downstream effects that partially overlap.

The landmark 2019 Dhaliwal study (British Journal of Dermatology) compared 0.5% bakuchiol twice daily vs 0.5% retinol once nightly over 12 weeks. Both groups showed:

- Reduced wrinkle depth
- Improved pigmentation
- No significant difference between groups

That's the data that launched the marketing. But the study has caveats:

- **Small sample** (44 subjects).
- **Not dose-matched for mechanism**, just for subjective endpoints.
- **Retinol was the weakest of the retinoid family** (retinoic acid is stronger; retinal is in between).
- **Only 12 weeks**; longer timeframes might separate the two.

No head-to-head against tretinoin (the gold-standard prescription retinoid) exists at the time of writing. So the fair statement is: bakuchiol is in the same conversation as over-the-counter retinol for some endpoints, at shorter timeframes, in a small trial.

## What it does well

- **Gentler onset.** No "retinization" phase (the 3-6 weeks of flaking/redness retinoids are known for).
- **Stable in sunlight.** Unlike retinoids, bakuchiol doesn't degrade or become photosensitizing.
- **Pregnancy-safe.** Whereas retinoids are contraindicated.
- **Pairs with vitamin C** in the same routine without stability conflicts.
- **Tolerated by sensitive skin**, rosacea-prone skin, and people who've failed retinoid trials for irritation reasons.

## Where it falls short

- **Not as strong as prescription tretinoin** for deep wrinkles, photodamage, or acne.
- **Results take longer** than OTC retinol in most user experience reports, despite the single trial showing parity.
- **Quality variance.** Bakuchiol concentration and stability in formulations is not consistent. Cheap bakuchiol products may under-deliver.

## Dose

Most effective formulations contain **0.5% to 1% bakuchiol**. Below 0.5%, data is thin. Above 1%, diminishing returns.

Look at the ingredient list: bakuchiol should be in the first third of the list. If it's near the end, the concentration is likely sub-therapeutic.

## Who should consider it

- **Pregnant or breastfeeding people** who want anti-aging but can't use retinoids.
- **People with sensitive skin** who've failed retinoid trials from irritation.
- **Rosacea-prone skin**, which tolerates bakuchiol better than most retinoids.
- **Anyone not ready for retinoid's 3-month adaptation** but wanting some anti-aging action.

## Who should skip it (in favor of retinoid)

- Anyone looking for the strongest anti-aging effect for their age. Tretinoin is still the ceiling.
- Anyone already tolerating retinoid without issue. The switch downgrades your potency.
- Anyone with significant hyperpigmentation. Tretinoin or adapalene plus hydroquinone or tranexamic acid works faster.

## How to use it

- **Morning or evening, or both.** Unlike retinoids, bakuchiol is stable in daylight.
- **Start every other day** to confirm tolerance; most people can ramp to daily within 2 weeks.
- **Pair with:** vitamin C, niacinamide, peptides, SPF.
- **Compatible with:** acids, PHAs (at reasonable frequency).

## The honest summary

Bakuchiol is a legitimate ingredient with real (if modest) anti-aging effects. It's an excellent tool in specific situations (pregnancy, sensitive skin, retinoid intolerance). It is not a "natural retinol" in the sense marketing implies. If you can tolerate a retinoid, a retinoid will do more.

## Buying notes

- **Ole Henriksen Transform Plus**, **BYBI Bakuchiol Booster**, **Paula's Choice 0.5% Retinol + 2% Bakuchiol** are well-formulated options.
- The Ordinary does not make a standalone bakuchiol product, but other budget brands (Inkey List) do.
- Check the INCI list for actual bakuchiol placement. A product with "bakuchiol extract" hiding at the end is selling the word, not the molecule.
