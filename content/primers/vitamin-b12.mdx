## What it is

Vitamin B12 (cobalamin) is a water-soluble B vitamin essential for DNA synthesis, red blood cell formation, and myelin maintenance in nerves. Only bacteria and archaea can synthesize it; animals accumulate it by eating contaminated plants or each other. In the modern food system, the only reliably rich sources are animal products (meat, eggs, dairy) and fortified foods (nutritional yeast, some plant milks).

Deficiency is slow and sneaky. Symptoms like fatigue, brain fog, pins-and-needles in hands and feet, and memory issues build gradually. By the time lab values flag it, damage to the nervous system can be partly irreversible.

## Who's at risk

- **Vegans and strict vegetarians** without fortified foods. The #1 at-risk group. Supplementation is not optional here; it's how you avoid neurological damage.
- **Adults over 50.** Atrophic gastritis is common and drops the intrinsic factor needed to absorb B12 from food.
- **Long-term proton pump inhibitor (PPI) or metformin users.** Both reduce B12 absorption.
- **People with pernicious anemia** (autoimmune loss of intrinsic factor). Requires lifelong supplementation.
- **Post-gastric-bypass patients.** Absorption is permanently altered.

## Forms

- **Methylcobalamin.** The active, coenzyme form. Best tolerated; often better in people with MTHFR variants. Most bioavailable for most people.
- **Cyanocobalamin.** Cheaper, more stable on the shelf. The body converts it to methyl- and adenosyl-cobalamin, but the conversion is imperfect in some genotypes. Still effective for most adults.
- **Adenosylcobalamin.** The other active form. Rarely sold alone; sometimes paired with methyl in "bioactive B12" products.
- **Hydroxocobalamin.** The injectable form; highest retention, used clinically.

For daily oral supplementation, **methylcobalamin** is the safer default. For cheap, long-shelf-life bulk use (like Nutricost's), cyanocobalamin is fine for most.

## Sublingual vs oral vs injection

- **Sublingual (dissolve under tongue).** Partial absorption through oral mucosa, bypassing some of the intrinsic-factor requirement. Studies consistently show decent serum B12 elevation. Worth the slight premium if you have absorption concerns.
- **Oral tablets.** Absorbed via intrinsic factor in the ileum. Dose-dependent: 1% of very large doses (1,000+ mcg) gets passively absorbed without intrinsic factor, which is why high-dose oral works even in pernicious anemia patients.
- **Injection.** Bypasses absorption entirely. Clinical-grade solution for severe deficiency. Your doctor handles this.

Practical takeaway: for most people, a 1,000 mcg methylcobalamin tablet (sublingual or swallowed) twice a week is sufficient. Vegans may benefit from 2,000 mcg 2-3 times per week.

## Dose

- **Maintenance:** 1,000-2,000 mcg methylcobalamin, 2 to 3 times per week. Or a 25-100 mcg daily dose if you prefer.
- **Correction of deficiency:** 1,000-2,000 mcg daily for 1-3 months, then re-test.
- **Vegan baseline:** 2,000 mcg 2-3x per week, indefinitely. Non-negotiable.

B12 is water-soluble and excess is urinated out. Toxicity from oral supplementation is effectively non-existent. The "bright yellow pee" you may notice on high-B-complex supplements is riboflavin (B2), not B12.

## Testing

- **Serum B12** is the baseline test. Aim for >400 pg/mL; below 200 is deficient.
- **Methylmalonic acid (MMA)** is more sensitive; elevates earlier than serum B12.
- **Homocysteine** is also a useful marker (elevates in B12/folate deficiency, linked to cardiovascular risk).

If you're symptomatic with borderline serum B12, ask for the MMA test.

## Timing

Morning, with or without food. B12 doesn't need a co-factor to be absorbed (beyond intrinsic factor for the bulk of the dose). Takes 1-3 hours to peak in plasma.

Don't take B12 (or B-complex) in the evening; some people find it mildly alerting.

## Common mistakes

- **Assuming "I eat meat, I'm fine."** Older adults and PPI users often aren't, despite regular meat intake.
- **Skipping supplementation as a vegan because "I eat nutritional yeast."** Yeast is often not fortified, or fortified inconsistently. Supplement regardless.
- **Over-relying on B-complex products.** Many B-complex blends have 6-12 mcg B12. That's maintenance for a fully absorbing adult, not correction of deficiency.
