## The short argument

Your skin is the organ you feed last. After brain, gut, and muscle get their macro- and micronutrients, the skin-specific enzymatic and structural work (collagen synthesis, ceramide production, antioxidant recycling) uses what's left. A small, targeted oral stack can push the supply side. That doesn't replace topicals, it supports them.

## The four that earn their spot

### 1. Hydrolyzed collagen peptides (10-20 g / day)

Bovine or marine, it doesn't matter much as long as the molecular weight is low (specific bioactive peptides like Pro-Hyp and Hyp-Gly need to survive digestion). Randomized trials show:

- Increased skin elasticity after 8 to 12 weeks
- Reduced perceived wrinkle depth
- Joint comfort improvements at 40+ years old

Take it with vitamin C in the same window. Morning coffee works (collagen dissolves in hot liquid without issue). Avoid "collagen water" bottled drinks; the dose is usually sub-clinical.

### 2. Vitamin C (500-1000 mg / day)

Vitamin C is a required cofactor for prolyl- and lysyl-hydroxylase, the enzymes that stabilize collagen's triple helix. Without enough vitamin C, oral collagen peptides get absorbed but then sit around un-deployed.

Dose math: the body tops out absorption around 200 mg at a time, so splitting (500 mg morning, 500 mg evening) is more effective than a single 1 g. Ascorbic acid is fine; liposomal is marketing unless you have a specific absorption problem.

### 3. Omega-3 (EPA + DHA, 2 g combined / day)

Skin is mostly lipid. Omega-3 fatty acids reduce inflammatory signaling in the skin, which shows up as less redness, calmer acne, and measurably lower trans-epidermal water loss. Particularly useful for:

- Acne-prone skin
- Atopic or eczema-adjacent skin
- Anyone who rarely eats fatty fish

Triglyceride-form (not ethyl ester) is better absorbed. Take with food.

### 4. Zinc (15 mg elemental / day)

Zinc is a cofactor for keratinization, wound healing, and sebum regulation. Clinically-dosed zinc supplementation is a legitimate acne intervention, especially for inflammatory (not comedonal) acne.

Forms: zinc picolinate and bisglycinate absorb best. Take with food to avoid nausea. Don't exceed 40 mg long-term without monitoring (copper balance becomes an issue).

## The optional ones

- **Biotin (2,500-5,000 mcg)**: genuinely helps if you are deficient, which almost no one is. The hair-and-nails marketing has no effect in normally-nourished adults. **Skip unless** you have a diagnosed deficiency or finger/toenail brittleness that won't resolve otherwise.
- **Hyaluronic acid oral (120-240 mg)**: some reasonable evidence for skin hydration after 8+ weeks. Cheap enough to try. Topical HA is still doing more work.
- **Astaxanthin (4-12 mg)**: strong antioxidant data and some photo-protective studies. Niche but legit.

## What doesn't work

- **Gummies with "skin-hair-nails" labels**: 2,000 mcg biotin, 20 mg vitamin C, and a lot of sugar. The biotin is usually the only ingredient at a functional dose, and most people don't need it.
- **Silica-based "beauty powders"**: weak evidence, expensive per dose.
- **Collagen peptides alone without vitamin C**: absorbs, but the downstream synthesis is vitamin-C-rate-limited.

## The baseline that matters most

- Sleep. Collagen turnover happens overnight.
- Daily broad-spectrum SPF. Nothing in any oral stack outpaces UV damage.
- Protein: 1.6 g / kg bodyweight / day minimum. Your skin is a protein structure.
- Enough water. Visibly flat skin at the end of the day is usually dehydration, not a product failure.
