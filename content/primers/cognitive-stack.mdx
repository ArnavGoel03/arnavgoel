## The honest premise

Most "nootropics" are marketing. A short list of supplements has real mechanistic evidence for cognitive benefit in healthy adults. The stack below is built entirely from that short list.

## The core four

### 1. Creatine monohydrate (5 g/day)

The best-studied sports supplement is quietly one of the best-studied cognitive supplements too. Creatine buffers ATP in high-demand tissue, and the brain is ATP-demanding. The cognitive signal is strongest in:

- Vegetarians and vegans (lower dietary baseline)
- During sleep deprivation (where ATP availability drops)
- Under high cognitive load

5 g daily, any time, with any meal. Don't bother with loading. Don't pay extra for HCL, ethyl ester, or Kre-Alkalyn; they are more expensive and not more effective.

### 2. Omega-3 (EPA + DHA, 2 g combined / day)

DHA is the dominant omega-3 in neural membranes. Low DHA is correlated with slower processing speed and worse mood. EPA has separate anti-inflammatory effects that show up in mood and motivation studies.

Buy fish oil or algae-derived triglyceride-form EPA/DHA, 2 g combined per day, with a fat-containing meal. Skip if mercury contamination is a concern: choose IFOS-certified brands.

### 3. Caffeine + L-Theanine (100-200 mg + 200 mg)

The combo is the most reliable acute focus lever on the list. Caffeine raises arousal; L-theanine smooths the sympathetic spike and adds alpha-wave activity. Subjective feel: alert but not jittery.

Tactical rules: last caffeine by noon for most people, 2 pm maximum. Skip on weekends or restrict to mornings-only to prevent tolerance.

### 4. Magnesium L-Threonate (2 g, containing ~144 mg elemental Mg)

The only magnesium form shown to meaningfully cross the blood-brain barrier (originally from MIT animal work). Signal is subtle: working memory, evening "decompression" feeling, sleep depth.

Take it in the early evening. It is not a replacement for glycinate for general-body magnesium needs; they target different compartments.

## The situational adds

- **Vitamin B12 (1,000-2,000 mcg methylcobalamin)** once or twice a week, especially if you eat little or no meat. B12 deficiency is underdiagnosed and expresses as cognitive sluggishness and neuropathy.
- **Choline (from eggs, or 250-500 mg alpha-GPC / CDP-choline)** if your diet is light on eggs and organ meat. Support for acetylcholine synthesis; subtle but real.
- **Bacopa monnieri (300 mg standardized extract)** for multi-month courses when you're learning a new skill. Effects take 8 to 12 weeks.

## What not to take

- **Noopept, piracetam, phenylpiracetam, modafinil.** Prescription or unregulated, limited long-term safety data, and the effect sizes in healthy adults are smaller than marketing suggests.
- **Pre-made "nootropic" blends** with 10+ ingredients at unknown doses.
- **Lion's mane for cognition.** Some interesting animal data, underpowered human trials. Safe, but don't expect much.

## The behaviors that outperform the stack

1. **Sleep.** 7-9 hours. The cognitive cost of one night of 4 hours of sleep is larger than the benefit of the entire stack above for a week.
2. **Zone 2 cardio**, 150+ minutes/week. Grows hippocampal volume. Genuinely more effective than any supplement for long-term cognition.
3. **Reading books**, not feeds. Sustained attention is a use-it-or-lose-it faculty.

Supplements stack on top of that. They don't substitute.
