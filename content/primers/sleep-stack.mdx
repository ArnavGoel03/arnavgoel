## The shape of a good sleep stack

Sleep is the first domino. If you fix it, everything else (focus, mood, training, skin) improves downstream. The supplements that have real evidence for sleep are a short list, and they work by different mechanisms, so stacking them makes sense as long as you know what each one is doing.

Three that earn their spot:

1. **Magnesium glycinate.** 300-400 mg elemental magnesium, 30-60 minutes before bed. Glycinate is the form that's gentle on the gut and delivers elemental magnesium into systemic circulation without the laxative effect of citrate or oxide. Magnesium supports GABA-A activity and smooths the switch from sympathetic to parasympathetic tone. Expect: easier sleep onset, less middle-of-the-night waking.

2. **L-Theanine.** 200 mg alongside or slightly before the magnesium. Increases alpha-wave activity without sedation, which blunts the "wired but tired" feeling from caffeine residue or a late work session. Pairs especially well on days you had coffee after 2 pm.

3. **Glycine.** 3 g about 30 minutes before bed. There's a small but consistent body of evidence that glycine lowers core body temperature and shortens time to stage 3 sleep. It also happens to be sweet (like real sugar), so you can stir it into water with zero palatability issues.

## What to add selectively

- **Ashwagandha (KSM-66 or Sensoril), 300-600 mg with dinner** on weeks when stress is the actual bottleneck. The evidence for cortisol reduction is legitimate for standardized extracts, less so for cheap root powder. Not needed daily.
- **Apigenin (chamomile extract), 50 mg** before bed if you want to try one extra thing. Weaker evidence but a real mechanism (binding at benzodiazepine sites). Skip if you're taking other sedatives.

## The melatonin problem

Standard US melatonin doses (3, 5, 10 mg) are **10 to 30 times** the physiologically active dose. Research grade is **0.3 mg**, taken 30 to 90 minutes before bed. At that dose, it cues circadian timing without a downstream desensitization story.

At US OTC doses, what you're actually getting:

- Residual grogginess the next morning
- Vivid, fragmented dreams
- Downregulation of endogenous melatonin if used nightly

Use melatonin sparingly: short trips across time zones, a single off-schedule night. If you find yourself needing it every evening, the problem is light exposure or stress, not a melatonin deficiency.

## What to avoid

- **Trazodone and "natural" blends with valerian** unless prescribed. The hangover effect isn't worth it.
- **CBD for sleep.** Underpowered evidence, highly variable product quality, expensive.
- **ZMA.** Zinc, magnesium, B6 in a proprietary ratio that's mostly marketing. Just take the components separately at real doses.

## The behavior upstream of any stack

No supplement outruns bad sleep hygiene. The non-negotiables, in rough order of impact:

1. Cool room, 65 to 68 degrees F
2. Last caffeine by noon if you're caffeine-sensitive, by 2 pm if not
3. Last meal 3 hours before bed
4. Screens dimmed or off for the last hour
5. Consistent sleep and wake times, including weekends

Supplements work on top of that baseline, not instead of it.
