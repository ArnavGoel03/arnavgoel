## The remineralization story

Tooth enamel is a biological mineral, primarily **hydroxyapatite** (Ca₁₀(PO₄)₆(OH)₂). It's constantly in a dynamic cycle of demineralization (acid attack from plaque bacteria and dietary acids) and remineralization (saliva redepositing calcium, phosphate, and hydroxide).

The job of a remineralizing toothpaste is to tip that balance toward redeposition. Two ingredient families genuinely do this:

- **Fluoride** (sodium fluoride, stannous fluoride, sodium monofluorophosphate)
- **Hydroxyapatite** (nano-HA, medical-grade; used in Japanese toothpastes for decades)

The internet treats these as rivals. The clinical evidence says they're different tools with different strengths, and both work.

## Fluoride (the long-established answer)

### How it works

Fluoride substitutes into the enamel crystal lattice, forming **fluorapatite** in place of hydroxyapatite. Fluorapatite is more acid-resistant than native enamel; it resists dissolution at a lower pH (around 4.5 vs 5.5). This is why fluoride toothpaste reduces cavity incidence so dramatically in population studies.

Fluoride also disrupts bacterial metabolism (inhibits enolase in cavity-causing bacteria), which reduces acid production at the source.

### Forms that matter

- **Sodium fluoride (NaF).** The gold standard. 1,000-1,500 ppm is the therapeutic range. Present in most US and UK toothpastes.
- **Stannous fluoride (SnF₂).** Better antimicrobial action than NaF. Adds gingivitis reduction and sensitivity benefit. Can cause mild staining over time (cosmetic, not permanent). Crest Pro-Health, Sensodyne Pronamel use stannous.
- **Sodium monofluorophosphate (MFP).** Older, less effective delivery vehicle. Present in some budget brands. Avoid if you have other options.

### Evidence

Population-level: fluoride toothpaste is associated with 20-40% reduction in cavity incidence vs non-fluoride. Community water fluoridation at 0.7 ppm has decades of observational support for reduced dental caries.

Individual-level: in adults with good baseline oral hygiene, the marginal benefit of fluoride toothpaste is smaller but still meaningful, especially for root caries (a risk as gums recede with age) and enamel erosion from acidic diets.

## Hydroxyapatite (the Japanese challenger)

### How it works

Nano-hydroxyapatite (nHA) particles deposit directly onto demineralized enamel, filling micro-defects and integrating with the existing mineral. It's a direct replacement model, not a chemical substitution. Because it's the same mineral as enamel, it doesn't need to trigger new crystal formation; it *is* the mineral.

### Evidence

Developed by NASA in the 1970s for astronaut tooth repair; commercialized in Japan (where it's approved as a cavity-prevention agent). Multiple controlled trials since 2014:

- **Non-inferior to fluoride** for cavity prevention in school-age children.
- Equal or superior for **enamel remineralization** in lab models.
- **Better for sensitivity**: fills the exposed dentinal tubules that cause sensitivity, in the same way desensitizing gels do.
- **Better tolerance**: no staining, no fluorosis risk (relevant for kids who swallow toothpaste).

The caveat: most of the strongest hydroxyapatite evidence comes from Japan. The US regulatory path is slower; nHA toothpaste is legally sold but not FDA-approved as a caries-prevention drug (it's cosmetic classification).

### Forms

- **Nano-hydroxyapatite (nHA).** The active form in modern toothpastes. Concentration typically 5-15%.
- **"Hydroxyapatite"** at micro (non-nano) size is common in older products and is less effective at integrating with enamel.

## Which to choose

### Default: fluoride
If you have:
- No hypersensitivity concerns
- No small children swallowing toothpaste
- A cavity-prone history
- Periodontal disease (stannous adds gingivitis benefit)

Use fluoride. It has the strongest evidence, the longest track record, and is cheapest.

### Switch to (or alternate with) hydroxyapatite if:
- You have chronic tooth sensitivity
- You're pregnant (some people prefer to avoid fluoride; evidence supports that fluoride at toothpaste concentrations is safe, but the nHA alternative is equally effective)
- You have young children who swallow toothpaste (fluorosis risk is real below age 6)
- You've had allergic/irritative reactions to fluoride products
- You simply prefer it; the evidence is now strong enough to stand on

### Or: alternate

No mechanistic conflict between the two. Some dentists recommend rotating morning (fluoride) and evening (hydroxyapatite) for the benefits of both.

## What actually matters more than the active

The ingredient in your toothpaste is a fraction of the equation. The rest:

1. **Brushing technique and time.** 2 minutes, twice a day, gentle circular motions. Pressure sensor on a modern electric toothbrush helps.
2. **Flossing.** Toothpaste can't reach between teeth. Floss or water pick daily.
3. **Diet.** Sugar frequency matters more than sugar quantity. Five small candies throughout the day damages more than one dessert after dinner.
4. **Saliva flow.** Hydration and chewing (sugar-free gum with xylitol) matter.
5. **Dental visits.** Every 6 months for cleaning. No home routine substitutes.

## What to avoid

- **Charcoal toothpastes.** Abrasive (RDA >100 in many products). Wears enamel over time.
- **DIY baking soda daily use.** Mild baking soda use is fine occasionally; daily at high concentrations is abrasive.
- **"Whitening" toothpastes with harsh abrasives.** The whitening is surface removal, not mineral restoration. Look for RDA under 75.
- **Oil pulling as a replacement for brushing.** As an adjunct, fine. As the main thing, a mechanical brush outperforms any oil by miles.

## Buying notes

- **Fluoride default:** Sensodyne Pronamel Multi-Action, Crest Pro-Health Advanced (stannous), Colgate Total (stannous).
- **Hydroxyapatite:** Boka, RiseWell, Dr. Brite (US); Apagard (Japanese, the original). Concentration matters; look for 10%+ nHA.
- **For kids under 6:** a rice-grain smear of fluoride toothpaste, or an nHA toothpaste.

## The ceiling

Either active, used correctly with good technique, puts you far ahead of average. The debate between fluoride and hydroxyapatite is real but the effect size is small compared to the effect of brushing properly for two minutes twice a day.
