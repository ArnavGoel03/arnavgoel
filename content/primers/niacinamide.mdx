## What it is

Niacinamide is a form of **vitamin B3**. In skincare it is one of the most broadly-studied actives, used at 2-10%. It does four well-documented things:

1. **Strengthens the skin barrier.** Increases ceramide synthesis, the skin holds onto moisture better.
2. **Regulates sebum.** Reduces the look of oily shine and enlarged pores over 4-8 weeks.
3. **Fades post-inflammatory pigmentation.** Not as fast as tranexamic acid or azelaic acid, but safer to stack.
4. **Reduces redness.** Useful for rosacea-prone skin and retinoid-induced irritation.

## The concentration question

- **2-5%**, barrier support. Fine even on sensitive skin.
- **5-10%**, the active-effect range. Most clinical studies land here.
- **10%+**, diminishing returns. Above 10% the evidence thins and the irritation risk rises. Some formulas cause a temporary warm flush (histamine response), not the same as an allergic reaction but often mistaken for one.

## The niacinamide + vitamin C myth

Old internet advice: "don't mix niacinamide and L-ascorbic acid, they cancel out." This comes from a 1960s study on raw chemicals at high heat, not on skin. Modern formulations are fine. You can layer them or use them at different times of day.

## How it pairs with other actives

- **With retinoids**: excellent. Niacinamide calms the barrier irritation retinoids cause.
- **With tranexamic acid**: standard combo for pigmentation. Most dark-spot serums pair the two.
- **With exfoliants (AHA/BHA)**: fine, but apply niacinamide last to buffer.

## What it does not do

It is not a retinoid, it won't remodel collagen or reverse photoaging. It is not an exfoliant, it won't unclog pores mechanically. It won't bleach pigmentation like hydroquinone. It is a supporting active, not a hero. Treat it that way.
