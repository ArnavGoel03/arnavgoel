## What it does (the three things)

Topical vitamin C, when it actually gets into your skin, does three useful jobs:

1. **Neutralizes reactive oxygen species** generated by UV and pollution. This is why it's the canonical morning antioxidant.
2. **Inhibits tyrosinase**, the enzyme that makes melanin. Consistently used vitamin C fades post-inflammatory hyperpigmentation over 2 to 4 months.
3. **Cofactor for collagen synthesis**, the same role it plays internally. Topical application pushes fibroblast activity in the upper dermis.

The tricky part: very few vitamin C products actually deliver the active molecule into skin at a useful dose.

## The gold standard: L-Ascorbic Acid (LAA)

L-ascorbic acid (LAA) is the form with the most clinical evidence. The constraints:

- **pH must be 3.5 or below** to penetrate the stratum corneum.
- **Concentration sweet spot is 10 to 20%**. Below 8%, effects disappear. Above 20%, absorption plateaus and irritation climbs.
- **Highly unstable in water and light.** Oxidizes to dehydroascorbic acid and then to brown, irritating breakdown products.
- **Synergizes with vitamin E (1%) and ferulic acid (0.5%).** The Duke formula (Skinceuticals CE Ferulic) doubles shelf stability and extends protective effect.

If your LAA serum has turned even slightly yellow or brown, it's oxidized. Use it up or throw it out; brown vitamin C can actively cause free-radical damage (the "pro-oxidant" switch).

## The stable derivatives (and why they're second-best)

When brand copy says "more stable, less irritating, gentler" vitamin C, they're almost always selling a derivative:

- **Tetrahexyldecyl Ascorbate (THD, also written THDC).** The best of the derivatives. Oil-soluble, penetrates well, stable at neutral pH. Clinical data weaker than LAA but real, especially at 5-10%.
- **Sodium Ascorbyl Phosphate (SAP).** Acne-specific signal; some antibacterial effect on C. acnes. Less brightening than LAA.
- **Magnesium Ascorbyl Phosphate (MAP).** Gentle, hydrating, weak.
- **Ascorbyl Glucoside (AG) and Ascorbyl Tetraisopalmitate (ATIP).** Convert to LAA in skin, but conversion rates are low (10-30%). Results underwhelming unless the concentration is very high (10%+).

Rule of thumb: if you can tolerate 15-20% LAA at pH ≤3.5, use it. If you can't (sensitive skin, rosacea, vitamin-C-induced flushing), THD 5-10% at neutral pH is the best fallback.

## When in your routine

**Morning.** Cleansed skin, before moisturizer and SPF. The antioxidant effect is protective against the UV you're about to get. The tyrosinase inhibition is ongoing either time of day.

Some people pair vitamin C with sunscreen morning and retinoid night, which is the classic efficacy stack.

## Layering rules

- **Works with:** Niacinamide (despite the internet myth about them canceling each other; they don't), sunscreen, peptides, most moisturizers.
- **Be careful with:** AHA/BHA acids (the combined low pH can irritate; use on alternate mornings if you want both). Benzoyl peroxide (oxidizes vitamin C; apply separately by hours, or use one AM and one PM).
- **Don't layer with:** Copper peptides (they can neutralize each other).

## Storage and shelf life

- **Opaque, airtight packaging.** Glass bottles with eyedroppers get too much air exposure over time. Pump bottles or airless vials are better.
- **Refrigerate if you live somewhere hot** or buy a large bottle you won't finish in 3 months.
- **Use within 3-6 months of opening** for LAA serums. Derivatives last 12+ months.

## What to expect

- Brightening effect on post-inflammatory marks: 6-12 weeks, noticeable but not dramatic.
- Firming: subtle, over 3-6 months. Easier to feel than to photograph.
- Stinging on first use at 15-20% LAA is normal for 2-5 minutes. If it persists or intensifies, lower the concentration or switch to a derivative.

## What not to do

- Don't use vitamin C as a spot treatment. It's a canvas, not a point application.
- Don't pair it with strong retinoid in the same morning routine; alternate mornings is fine, same-time layering invites irritation.
- Don't buy a vitamin C serum in clear glass from Amazon unless the seller has fast turnover. Oxidation in shipping is real.
