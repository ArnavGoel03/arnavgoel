## What a peptide is

A peptide is a short chain of amino acids, shorter than a full protein. Skincare peptides are typically 2 to 10 amino acids long, small enough to penetrate the stratum corneum and reach living cells in the upper epidermis.

In skincare, peptides do one of three things, depending on their structure:

1. **Signal** fibroblasts or keratinocytes to do something (make more collagen, repair the barrier, regulate pigmentation).
2. **Carry** trace metals into skin (notably copper, which is a cofactor for wound-healing enzymes).
3. **Block** neurotransmitter release at neuromuscular junctions to temporarily relax muscle (the "topical Botox" story, which is weaker than marketing suggests).

Most "peptide moisturizers" don't specify which peptides they use at what concentration. The ones that do are usually the ones worth paying for.

## The peptides with real data

### Signaling peptides

**Matrixyl (Palmitoyl Pentapeptide-4)**
- One of the most-studied signaling peptides.
- Fragment of procollagen I, which signals fibroblasts to produce more collagen when detected.
- 3-5% in a well-formulated serum shows measurable improvements in wrinkle depth at 12 weeks.

**Matrixyl 3000 (Palmitoyl Tripeptide-1 + Palmitoyl Tetrapeptide-7)**
- A newer formulation targeting both collagen synthesis and inflammatory pathways.
- Good evidence for fine lines and firmness.

**Palmitoyl Tripeptide-38 (Matrixyl Synthe'6)**
- Claims to stimulate multiple collagen types (I, III) and laminin. Strong in-vitro data, modest in-vivo.

**GHK-Cu (Copper Tripeptide-1)**
- Both a signaling and a carrier peptide. Historically in The Ordinary's Buffet + Copper Peptides 1%.
- Evidence for wound healing, collagen stimulation, and anti-inflammatory action.
- Special caveat: **don't layer with vitamin C or strong acids in the same application** (copper redox can interfere).

### Carrier peptides

**Copper peptides (beyond GHK-Cu)**
- Deliver copper, a cofactor for lysyl oxidase (collagen/elastin cross-linking) and superoxide dismutase (antioxidant enzymes).
- Most active in post-procedure or injured skin.

### Neuromuscular peptides (the weakest category)

**Argireline (Acetyl Hexapeptide-8)**
- Mimics the N-terminal end of SNAP-25; theoretically blocks acetylcholine release at neuromuscular junctions.
- Reduces expression-line depth modestly in clinical trials (around 10-17% at high concentrations over 30 days).
- **Not a Botox substitute.** The effect size is small and temporary.

**Syn-Ake (Dipeptide Diaminobutyroyl Benzylamide Diacetate)**
- Similar mechanism, similar modest effects.

Be skeptical of marketing that compares any topical peptide to injectable neuromodulators. The delivery and effect sizes aren't comparable.

## Barrier-support peptides

**Palmitoyl Tripeptide-5, Tripeptide-10 Citrulline, etc.**
- Various proprietary blends targeting barrier lipid synthesis and keratinocyte function.
- Harder to evaluate individually because they're rarely sold standalone.

## What peptides don't do

- **Work overnight.** Collagen synthesis is slow. Expect 8-12 weeks minimum for visible results.
- **Beat retinoids for firming.** Retinoid is the ceiling topical for collagen remodeling. Peptides are complementary, not a replacement.
- **Fix sun damage already done.** Prevention (SPF) plus slow remodeling (peptides, retinoid, vitamin C) is the real playbook.

## How to use them

- **Concentration matters.** Most peptides need 3-10% to reach efficacious doses. A "peptide complex" listed at the end of an ingredient list is decorative.
- **Morning or evening:** both work. Mornings if you're also doing retinoid at night. Evenings if you prefer.
- **Pair with:** moisturizer, niacinamide, SPF (AM).
- **Be cautious with:** strong acids (pH drops can denature peptides), vitamin C alongside copper peptides specifically.

## Realistic expectations

- **Fine lines:** modest improvement over 12 weeks.
- **Firmness and texture:** subtle, over 3-6 months.
- **Post-procedure healing:** notable acceleration with copper peptides.
- **Expression lines (forehead, crow's feet):** mild softening, not paralysis.

## Who should prioritize peptides

- Retinoid-intolerant people who want collagen support.
- People already doing the full active routine (retinoid, vitamin C, SPF) who want incremental gains.
- Post-procedure healing.
- Anyone over 40 looking to layer multiple anti-aging mechanisms.

## Who shouldn't bother yet

- Anyone not wearing daily SPF. Peptides are extra credit; sunscreen is the foundation.
- Anyone hoping a topical will replace neuromodulator treatments.
- Anyone not willing to commit to 3 months of consistent use.

## Buying notes

- **The Ordinary's "Matrixyl 10% + HA"** is the budget benchmark for Matrixyl exposure.
- **Dennis Gross Advanced Correction Peptide** and similar dermatology-brand peptide serums give you clinical-grade formulations at premium pricing.
- **K-beauty** is increasingly strong in peptides. Medicube, Anua, Torriden all have credible peptide products.
- **Avoid** any product where peptides are the only marketing claim but the concentrations aren't specified.
