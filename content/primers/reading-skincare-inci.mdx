## What INCI is

INCI (International Nomenclature of Cosmetic Ingredients) is the standardized naming convention for cosmetic ingredients. Every product sold legally in the US, EU, and most of Asia lists ingredients on the back in INCI format, in a specific order.

The order is the most useful piece of information on the package, and most people ignore it.

## The ordering rule

Ingredients are listed **in descending order by weight**, with one exception: ingredients under 1% of the formula can be listed in any order.

That means:

- The first 3-5 ingredients tell you what the product actually is.
- After that, the order drops off sharply.
- Past the 1% line, everything is under 1% and order is arbitrary.

## Finding the 1% line (the key skill)

You don't see a literal line on the label. You have to identify it from context. The indicators:

### Typical ingredients that sit right around 1%
- **Xanthan gum** or **carbomer** (thickeners): usually 0.1-0.5%.
- **Sodium hyaluronate** (as a feature, not as the active): often 0.1-1%.
- **Phenoxyethanol** or **methylisothiazolinone** (preservatives): 0.5-1%.
- **Fragrance / parfum:** often 0.5-2%.
- **Tocopherol** (vitamin E): 0.05-0.5%.
- **Disodium EDTA:** 0.05-0.2%.

When you see a cluster of these, you've hit the 1% line. **Anything listed after that is in trace amounts** regardless of what the marketing says.

### Example: a suspicious "retinol serum"

Imagine this ingredient list:

> Aqua, Glycerin, Propanediol, Butylene Glycol, Niacinamide, Sodium Hyaluronate, Xanthan Gum, Phenoxyethanol, **Retinol**, Tocopherol, Fragrance.

Retinol appears after Phenoxyethanol and Xanthan Gum. Both are almost certainly below 1%, which means the retinol is below 1% too, probably below 0.1%. The product is marketing retinol but the active is essentially decorative.

Compare to:

> Aqua, Propanediol, **Retinol**, Glycerin, Niacinamide, Squalane, Caprylic/Capric Triglyceride, Phenoxyethanol, Tocopherol.

Retinol is third on the list, well above the 1% line. This is a real retinol product.

## Common trap: "Contains X" claims on the front

Brands lean heavily on front-label claims ("with retinol," "enriched with collagen," "infused with bakuchiol"). These are unregulated marketing phrases; there is no legal minimum concentration implied.

**The INCI list is the truth.** The front label is a pitch.

## When concentration is disclosed

Some brands publish actual percentages:

- **The Ordinary** lists percentages in product names and descriptions ("Niacinamide 10% + Zinc 1%," "Retinol 0.5% in Squalane").
- **Paula's Choice** publishes concentration details on product pages.
- **Some K-beauty brands** print percentages on the package (Anua often does).

If you can confirm concentration, you can compare products directly. A 10% niacinamide product is meaningfully different from an "infused with niacinamide" product at 0.3%.

## Which ingredients actually need to be above the 1% line

To work, these actives need to be listed **before** the 1% cluster:

- **Vitamin C (L-AA):** needs 10-20%, must be above the 1% line by a wide margin.
- **Niacinamide:** effective at 2-10%.
- **Retinol / retinal:** 0.1-1%; formulation quality matters as much as concentration.
- **AHAs (glycolic, lactic):** 5-20% for leave-on products.
- **Salicylic acid (BHA):** 1-2% for leave-on.
- **Azelaic acid:** 10-20%.
- **Bakuchiol:** 0.5-1%.
- **Hyaluronic acid:** works at 1-2%; higher isn't dramatically better.
- **Peptides:** varies by peptide; the Matrixyl class wants 3-10%.
- **Centella / Cica:** 2-5% as a soothing agent.

Which ingredients are fine below the 1% line:

- **Fragrance / essential oils** (and ideally absent, not just low).
- **Preservatives** (functional at <1%).
- **Texture modifiers** (gums, carbomers).
- **Antioxidant stabilizers** (tocopherol, ascorbyl palmitate).

## Ingredients that signal formulation quality

Presence of certain ingredients tells you the product was formulated thoughtfully:

### Positive signals
- **Ceramides (usually ceramide NP / AP / EOP) with cholesterol and fatty acids.** Barrier-repair intent.
- **Pentylene glycol / propanediol** (sustainable humectants) over **propylene glycol**.
- **Specific peptides by INCI name** (Palmitoyl Pentapeptide-4, rather than "peptide complex").
- **Third-party or clinical-research references on the brand's site** for actives.

### Neutral
- **Parabens** (methylparaben, propylparaben): effective preservatives, safe at used concentrations despite the backlash. Not a quality signal either way.
- **Sulfates** (SLS, SLES) in cleansers: fine for body, often too drying for face.
- **Silicones** (dimethicone, cyclopentasiloxane): create smooth feel, don't clog pores in most people. Not inherently bad.

### Negative signals
- **Denatured alcohol (SD Alcohol, Alcohol Denat.) in the top 5 ingredients** of a leave-on product. Drying.
- **Mineral oil as a primary ingredient of a treatment product.** Fine in an occlusive like Aquaphor. Questionable in a "serum."
- **Very long fragrance blends** with multiple essential oils (linalool, limonene, geraniol, etc.) in products for sensitive skin.
- **Microbeads or plastic microparticles** for exfoliation. Environmental and ineffective.

## Pack format matters as much as ingredients

A great formula in a bad container delivers less:

- **Clear glass + dropper** for vitamin C or retinoid: air and light exposure every use. The product degrades within weeks.
- **Jar + fingers** for any active product: contamination every time you dip.
- **Opaque pump with airless chamber**: gold standard for stable active products.
- **Aluminum tubes**: great for actives; metal blocks light and the tube seals as you squeeze.

## The 30-second evaluation

For any product you're considering:

1. **Front label**: what's the marketing claim?
2. **INCI**: where in the list is the named active? Above the 1% line?
3. **Brand page**: does it publish a concentration? If not, that silence is usually intentional.
4. **Package**: is the active protected from light and air?
5. **Price per active**: compare to a known-good product with the same active at the same concentration.

If the named active is in the bottom half of the INCI list, the product is probably marketing the ingredient without delivering it. Save your money.

## The exception: multi-active formulas

Some products combine multiple actives at reasonable concentrations. In those cases, each active may sit lower on the list because the total "active section" is split among several ingredients. Examples:

- A well-formulated barrier moisturizer with ceramides, niacinamide, and panthenol at clinically relevant concentrations each.
- A peptide serum with 3-4 different peptides at complementary doses.

When multiple known-good actives appear together above the 1% line, the product is genuinely multi-active. When five "hero ingredients" each appear below the 1% line, you have a fairy dust product.

## One last rule

The shorter the INCI list, the easier it is to evaluate. A 15-ingredient product is auditable. A 45-ingredient product with three proprietary complexes is almost always half marketing.
