## What it is

Ashwagandha (Withania somnifera) is a shrub in the nightshade family, native to India and parts of North Africa. The active compounds are withanolides, a group of steroidal lactones concentrated in the root (and, to a lesser extent, the leaf).

In Ayurvedic practice it's classified as a rasayana, a rejuvenative. In modern clinical trials it's primarily studied as an adaptogen: a compound that blunts the physiological stress response without sedating you.

## Which extract matters

The effect size in a study depends heavily on which preparation was used. Two standardized extracts dominate the clinical literature:

- **KSM-66 (Ixoreal Biomed)**. Root-only, standardized to 5% withanolides. The most-studied extract. Typical clinical dose: 300-600 mg/day. Balanced profile, not sedating, usable all day.
- **Sensoril (Natreon)**. Root and leaf, standardized to 10% withanolides. More sedating due to higher withanolide A content. Better for evening / sleep applications. Clinical dose: 125-250 mg/day.

Unstandardized "raw ashwagandha root powder" can range anywhere from 0.1% to 3% withanolides depending on the source, the soil, and the processing. Some is excellent. Some is sawdust. Without third-party testing, you're guessing.

## What it actually does (replicated effects)

- **Cortisol reduction.** Multiple 8-week RCTs show 15-30% reductions in serum cortisol at KSM-66 600 mg/day in chronically-stressed populations.
- **Subjective stress and anxiety scores** drop, with medium effect sizes in standard self-report instruments (PSS, HAM-A).
- **Sleep quality** improves, probably indirectly via cortisol reduction. Time-to-sleep shortens modestly.
- **Strength and muscle gains** in trained men at 600 mg/day (small effect, larger in untrained subjects).
- **Testosterone** increases modestly in men with low baseline testosterone. Not a replacement for TRT at clinical hypogonadism levels.

## What it probably doesn't do

- Turn a healthy person into a calm, focused, muscle-bound superhuman. The marketing here is ahead of the data.
- Work acutely. Effects take 2 to 4 weeks to build; most trials are 8 weeks long. If you take it once and expect to feel different, you won't.

## Who should consider it

- Anyone with a high-cortisol lifestyle (shift work, chronic deadlines, overtraining).
- Mild anxiety or sleep-onset issues not severe enough for medication.
- Athletes during heavy training blocks (recovery signal).

## Who should skip or be careful

- **Pregnant or breastfeeding people.** Traditional use contraindicates it, and modern safety data is thin.
- **Autoimmune conditions (Hashimoto's, lupus, RA).** Ashwagandha has immunomodulatory effects that can tip thyroid antibodies or disease activity. Talk to your rheumatologist or endocrinologist first.
- **On thyroid medication.** Ashwagandha can raise T3/T4 modestly and shift your dosing.
- **On sedatives, SSRIs, or benzodiazepines.** Additive effects possible.

## Dosing protocols

- **Daily stress:** KSM-66 300 mg twice a day, morning and evening, with meals, for 8-12 weeks. Then re-evaluate whether you still need it.
- **Sleep-focused:** Sensoril 125 mg with dinner.
- **Athletic recovery:** KSM-66 600 mg post-workout or with the largest meal.

Rotate off for 1 to 2 weeks every 3 to 4 months. There's no hard evidence that tolerance develops, but there's also no evidence against taking breaks.

## The honest ceiling

Ashwagandha is the most legitimate adaptogen on the market. It also isn't magic. If your cortisol problem is "I sleep 5 hours, drink 4 coffees, and work 70 hours a week," a 600 mg capsule isn't the intervention. Fix the upstream causes; ashwagandha smooths the edges of a baseline that's already in reasonable shape.
