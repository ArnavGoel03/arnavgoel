## What it is

Creatine is an amino-acid derivative your body makes from glycine, arginine, and methionine. About 95% is stored in skeletal muscle as phosphocreatine, where it donates a phosphate group to rapidly regenerate ATP during short, intense effort.

Supplementation saturates muscle phosphocreatine stores beyond what food alone delivers (~1 g/day from meat and fish). The effect is measurable, reproducible, and boring. Which is why it's the single most-studied ergogenic aid in the history of sports science.

## Forms (and why you should only ever buy one)

- **Monohydrate.** The form used in virtually every positive trial. Cheapest. The answer.
- **HCL, ethyl ester, Kre-Alkalyn, buffered creatine, nitrate, malate.** More expensive. Not more effective. In some cases (ethyl ester) actively less stable in solution.

If a label says anything other than "creatine monohydrate," you're paying extra for marketing. Micronized monohydrate is fine (it just dissolves faster); it's still monohydrate.

## Dose

- **3 to 5 g per day, every day.**
- No loading phase required. Loading (20 g/day for 5-7 days) just saturates stores ~2 weeks faster. The long-term outcome is identical. Skip it unless you have a competition in 10 days.
- Timing doesn't matter. With any meal, any time of day.
- Take it on rest days too. Maintenance, not triggered dosing.

Four Nutricost capsules = 3 g. One rounded teaspoon of bulk powder = 5 g. Either is fine.

## What it actually does

### In the gym
- 5 to 15% more reps near max effort
- Small but consistent strength and hypertrophy gains over 8+ weeks
- Temporary ~1-2 kg weight increase from intramuscular water (not fat, not a problem)

### In the brain (less hyped, real)
- Measurable cognitive benefit in vegetarians and vegans, whose baseline brain creatine is lower
- Preservation of cognitive performance under sleep deprivation
- Emerging but not yet settled evidence for mood benefit in certain depressed populations

## What it doesn't do

- Build muscle without training. It's a substrate for effort, not a growth signal.
- Damage kidneys in healthy adults. This myth is stubborn; the clinical evidence does not support it. (If you have pre-existing renal disease, talk to your doctor.)
- Cause hair loss. The one DHT study that spawned this claim has not been replicated.
- Cause cramps or dehydration. Also not supported by the literature.

## Who actually benefits most

- Strength and power athletes (obvious).
- Endurance athletes during high-intensity intervals.
- Vegetarians and vegans (low dietary baseline).
- Adults over 50 looking to preserve muscle mass.
- Anyone under chronic sleep deficit (cognitive benefit).

## Who benefits least

- People who already eat 2+ servings of red meat per day (mostly saturated from diet).
- People who don't train. The muscle benefit has nothing to claim.
