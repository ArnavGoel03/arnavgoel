## What the barrier is

The outer 15-20 μm of your skin is a brick-and-mortar wall. The bricks are dead skin cells (corneocytes); the mortar is a mix of three lipids in a specific ratio:

- **Ceramides** (~50%)
- **Cholesterol** (~25%)
- **Free fatty acids** (~10-15%)

When that mortar thins out, from over-cleansing, harsh actives, weather, or aging, water escapes and irritants get in. This is what people mean by a "damaged" or "compromised" barrier: tight, flaky, stingy, red, reactive.

## What ceramide products do

Topical ceramides don't migrate deep into the skin. What they do is patch the outer mortar layer temporarily, reducing water loss while the skin rebuilds its own.

Two things to know:

1. **Ceramides work best in a full lipid mix.** A product that lists ceramides alongside cholesterol and fatty acids (the "1:1:1" family) is using the right ratio.
2. **Ceramides are expensive to stabilize.** Cheap "ceramide moisturizers" often have trace amounts. Mid-range dermatology brands (CeraVe, La Roche-Posay, Dr. Jart+) tend to have enough to matter.

## When you need them

- Post-procedure (laser, peels, extractions)
- Retinoid or exfoliant introduction weeks
- Dry climate, overheated offices, winter, travel
- Any day your skin feels tight after cleansing

## How to pair

- **With humectants** (hyaluronic acid, glycerin): humectant first, ceramide moisturizer sealing. The humectant pulls water in; the ceramides lock it.
- **With retinoids**: ceramide moisturizer immediately after retinoid on retinoid nights.
- **With occlusives** (petrolatum, squalane, shea): ceramide in the middle, occlusive on top if the climate is dry enough.

## What to avoid

- Foaming cleansers with sulfates + high-pH soap bases. They strip the lipids you're trying to rebuild.
- Daily AHAs + BHAs + retinoids in the same evening. Rotate.
- Double-digit actives on compromised barrier weeks. Go simple until the sting subsides.
