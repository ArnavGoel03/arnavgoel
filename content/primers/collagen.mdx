## What it is

Collagen is the most abundant protein in your body, the structural scaffold of skin, tendons, ligaments, bone, and cartilage. Dietary or supplemental collagen is, at one level, just a protein: gets digested to amino acids and di/tri-peptides like everything else.

The "collagen is special" claim depends on the fact that a small but meaningful fraction of collagen-derived peptides (notably Pro-Hyp and Hyp-Gly, which contain hydroxyproline) survive digestion intact and appear in plasma. These specific peptides act as signaling molecules to fibroblasts and chondrocytes, upregulating local collagen synthesis in skin and connective tissue.

That's the mechanism. Whether the effect is large enough to see in a mirror is what the trials actually answer.

## Forms

- **Hydrolyzed collagen peptides.** Pre-digested, low molecular weight (typically 2-5 kDa). Dissolves in hot or cold liquid without setting. The form in most trials. Buy this.
- **Gelatin.** Same source, less broken down, higher molecular weight. Sets in cold water. Slightly better satiety from a meal; worse for a drink. Bioactive peptide story is similar but not identical.
- **Undenatured type II collagen (UC-II, 40 mg).** Different beast entirely. Used at micro-doses for joint problems via an oral tolerance mechanism, not as a building block.

Type matters less than most marketing suggests. "Type I for skin, II for joints, III for blood vessels" is mostly copy. Hydrolyzed bovine or marine collagen covers the same ground.

## Dose

**10 to 20 g of hydrolyzed peptides per day.** Most positive trials sit in the 10-15 g range. You can go higher without issue (it's just protein).

**Take with vitamin C (500+ mg) in the same window.** Prolyl-hydroxylase, the enzyme that stabilizes collagen's triple helix, requires vitamin C as a cofactor. Collagen peptides without enough circulating ascorbate get absorbed, but downstream synthesis is rate-limited. This is the single most-skipped detail.

Timing within the day doesn't matter for the skin story. For joint rehab, some trials dose pre-training.

## What it actually does (replicated effects)

### Skin
- Increased skin elasticity (measured by cutometer) after 8-12 weeks.
- Improved skin hydration.
- Reduced perceived wrinkle depth in middle-aged populations.
- Effect is real but modest. Expect "noticeably less dry, slightly firmer." Don't expect a Photoshop-grade transformation.

### Joints
- Reduced joint pain in mild osteoarthritis (hydrolyzed peptides at 10 g).
- Faster recovery from tendon injuries when paired with vitamin C and intermittent loading.
- Better pain scores in athletes with activity-related joint discomfort.

### Hair and nails
- Weaker evidence than skin or joints. Some people notice nail growth improvement; most trials are small or poorly-controlled.

## What it doesn't do

- Outperform a high-protein diet on a per-gram basis for muscle-building. It's a low-quality protein in the muscle-growth sense (poor amino acid profile, low leucine). Don't count it as your protein.
- Replace topical skincare. Oral collagen adjusts supply; topical skincare adjusts the barrier, active ingredients, and sun protection.
- Work without adequate total protein. If your overall protein intake is 0.8 g/kg/day, fix that first; collagen isn't the missing piece.

## Practical buying notes

- Any reputable bovine or marine hydrolyzed collagen is fine. Vital Proteins, Great Lakes, Bulk Supplements, Sports Research: all comparable.
- Marine collagen has a smaller peptide size on average and some people prefer it; bovine is cheaper per gram. No compelling reason to pay the marine premium unless you eat no beef.
- "Collagen coffee creamer" products with 2 g of collagen per scoop are sub-clinical. Use real dosing.
- Flavored products with sucrose or sucralose are fine if they get you to take it daily. Palatability adherence beats purity.

## Bonus: why vitamin C alone won't do it

Just taking vitamin C doesn't build more collagen out of thin air. It only accelerates the stabilization step when the substrates (glycine, proline, hydroxyproline from food or peptides) are there. Think of collagen peptides as bricks, vitamin C as mortar. You need both.
