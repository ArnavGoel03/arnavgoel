## What makes Thorne different

Thorne is the brand that shows up repeatedly when people ask "what's the most conservative, best-sourced version of X?", because:

1. **NSF Certified for Sport.** Every batch is tested for banned substances. Matters for competitive athletes; doesn't matter technically for most lifters, but is a useful signal of manufacturing discipline.
2. **Creapure® as the source.** German-made creatine monohydrate held to tighter purity specs than most commodity creatine. Negligible performance difference vs standard monohydrate, the value is trust in the raw material, not a different molecule.
3. **No additives.** One ingredient on the label: creatine monohydrate.

## Dose

One scoop (~5 g) per day, every day. Mixes cleanly in water, Thorne's micronization is notably good; the powder dissolves faster than most generic creatines before it falls out of suspension.

## Price reality

This is the most expensive mainstream creatine you can buy, and it's still delivering the same 5 g of creatine monohydrate as a Nutricost tub that costs a fraction per gram. The premium is for the NSF certification, the Creapure sourcing, and Thorne's QC reputation, not a more-potent product.

If you're a competitive athlete subject to banned-substance testing, or you care strongly about single-source European raw material, Thorne justifies its price. Otherwise, the cheaper monohydrate is doing the same thing in your muscles.

## Regional note

Thorne is US-centric. Amazon India stocks selected Thorne SKUs intermittently; Amazon UK less reliably. The cheaper international substitutes (Myprotein, MuscleBlaze) use commodity monohydrate but are not NSF Sport tested.
