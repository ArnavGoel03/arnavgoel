## What chia seeds actually contain

Per 2-tablespoon (~28 g) serving:

- **~5 g plant protein**, complete amino acid profile, rare for a seed
- **~11 g fiber**, mostly soluble, which is what makes them gel in liquid
- **~5 g omega-3 (ALA)**, the plant form of omega-3

The ALA caveat is important. The omega-3 story people quote, lower triglycerides, cardiovascular protection, mostly comes from EPA and DHA, the marine forms found in fish oil. Your body converts ALA to EPA/DHA at a rate of roughly 5-10% for EPA and under 1% for DHA. So a serving of chia provides a measurable dose of ALA but a much smaller *effective* EPA/DHA dose than an actual fish oil capsule.

That doesn't make chia useless. The fiber, satiety, and protein contribution are the actual everyday value. The omega-3 is a small bonus.

## How I use them

Two tablespoons soaked in a jar of oat or whole milk overnight, eaten next morning with fruit. The gel texture is the whole appeal, a sense of fullness from a small volume, without being heavy.

## What it's not

- **Not a fish oil replacement.** If your goal is EPA/DHA for cardiovascular or cognitive outcomes, fish oil or algae oil delivers it directly. Chia doesn't.
- **Not a protein source that stands on its own.** 5 g per serving is a supplement to a protein plan, not the base of one.
- **Not a weight-loss food in isolation.** The satiety is real but it's fiber doing the work, not some chia-specific property.

## Regional note

Viva Naturals is a US/Canada brand; chia as an ingredient is widely available globally under many brands, so readers outside the US can substitute any organic chia without losing anything.
