## What 24% actually means

L-ascorbic acid stops becoming meaningfully more effective above ~20% — additional concentration mostly increases irritation without adding benefit. 24% is the practical ceiling. If your skin tolerates it, you're getting the full effect. If it doesn't, you'll know on the first application.

## Routine

Mornings only, on bare skin after cleansing, before moisturizer and SPF. SPF is non-negotiable with vitamin C — both because of photosensitivity and because the antioxidant effect is amplified under sunscreen.

## What I noticed

Old post-acne marks along the jawline lightened over six weeks. No dramatic transformation, but a real shift in the marks I'd written off as permanent.

## Why the rating isn't higher

L-ascorbic acid has a fundamental stability problem — once oxidized, it's not just less effective, it can be mildly pro-inflammatory. By month two the bottle is noticeably amber, which is the clock running out. If you don't use it consistently, you waste a lot of product.

## Verdict

Effective for the right skin type, fussy enough that I'd probably switch to a more stable derivative (sodium ascorbyl phosphate, ascorbyl glucoside) if I were starting over.
